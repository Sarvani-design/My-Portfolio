Fintech Mobile UI AI-Assisted Solo Project Concept

Flow — Personal Finance
That Doesn't Feel Like Finance

A conversational budgeting app designed for young professionals who find traditional finance tools cold, overwhelming, and built for someone else.

Solo UI/UX Designer
2–3 weeks
Google Stitch, Figma, Claude
iOS Mobile App
Concept — no testing yet

The Problem

Most budgeting apps assume you already know how to budget. They greet you with spreadsheet-style dashboards, categories you have to manually fill in, and language borrowed from accounting software. For a 24-year-old getting their first real salary — they feel instantly alienating.

The result? People download them, poke around for a day, then abandon them. Not because they don't care about money — but because the tools make them feel like they're doing it wrong.

"Money management apps feel like they were built for my parents. I don't have an investment portfolio — I just want to stop running out of money before the 25th."

The design challenge: how might we make personal finance feel like a conversation with a financially savvy friend, rather than a form you're submitting to a bank?

Who This Is For

Young professionals, 22–30 years old. They have a salary — maybe their first real one — and they're figuring out what to do with it. They're not broke, but they're not investing either. They live in a grey zone between "I'll sort it out later" and genuine financial anxiety.

Key traits of this user:

They're financially self-aware but action-paralyzed

They know they should save. They don't know where to start. A blank budget template doesn't help — it just emphasizes how unprepared they feel.

They respond to personality, not formality

They use apps like Duolingo and Notion that feel like tools built for humans. Finance shouldn't feel different.

They want to be met where they are

Not where a financial advisor thinks they should be. Their baseline could be "I have no idea what I spend" — and that's a valid starting point.

My Process

I used a hybrid approach: AI for ideation and speed, my own judgment for every design decision. Here's how it actually worked:

1

Direction from Google Stitch

I used Google Stitch to generate the initial concept and overall direction — the idea of a conversational onboarding, the name "Flow," the tone. Stitch gave me a strong starting point but not a finished design.

2

Choosing what to keep

I reviewed everything Stitch generated and made deliberate choices: which screens served the user journey, which interactions felt forced, which visual directions felt authentic vs. generic. I cut a lot.

3

Refinement in Figma with Claude

I used Claude to refine and elevate specific elements — sharpening the copy on onboarding questions so they felt genuinely warm (not sarcastic-warm), tightening the information hierarchy on the dashboard, and thinking through the progression of screens.

4

Building out the UI system

I developed a consistent visual language — color tokens, type scale, card patterns — so every screen felt like it belonged to the same product. Light and dark themes were both designed end-to-end.

The Design

Flow's experience is built around two core moments: a conversational onboarding that earns trust before asking for data, and a dashboard that shows spending in context rather than raw numbers.

Onboarding Flow — Meeting the User First

Instead of asking "enter your monthly budget," Flow starts by asking questions a friend would ask. The tone is warm and direct — no jargon, no judgment.

What brings you?

Conversational onboarding over blank-slate setup

The first screen the user sees is: "Hey [Name]! What brings you to Flow?" followed by friendly options — not a form. This reduces friction and immediately signals: this app is going to talk to you like a person.

Salary before budget

Most apps ask you to set a budget before knowing your income. Flow asks about take-home salary first and asks how comfortable you are with money stuff — acknowledging that financial literacy varies. The budget gets built around the user's reality, not an ideal.

Celebration micro-moments

When users complete setup or hit a savings milestone, Flow shows a "You actually did it!" screen. Small wins feel big when the app acknowledges them. This isn't just delight design — it reinforces behavior that benefits the user.

Light + dark theme as equal citizens

The dark theme isn't an afterthought. Both themes were designed in parallel with the same level of care — because young professionals switch between them based on context (bedtime money-checking is a real use case).

What I Would Test First

This is a concept — no user testing has been done yet. If I were to take this into validation, here's where I'd focus:

→

Does the conversational tone land or feel patronizing?

Warmth is a fine line. "Hey Raghav!" might feel great to some users and infantilizing to others. I'd test the onboarding copy with 5–8 users in the target age range.

→

Does the dashboard give people the right mental model?

The ₹23,800 overview with bar charts assumes users understand spending categories at a glance. I'd run a comprehension test on the dashboard before assuming it works.

→

Drop-off in the onboarding funnel

Each question is a potential exit point. Tracking where users abandon the flow would tell me which questions feel too intrusive or unclear.

Reflection

This project changed how I think about AI tools in a design workflow. Google Stitch didn't design Flow — it gave me a starting direction. The difference matters. Every screen I shipped went through my own lens: does this serve the user? Does this tone feel right? Is this hierarchy actually readable?

AI sped up ideation by maybe 60%. But it didn't replace the judgment calls — which screens to include, which copy felt authentic vs. generated, when a feature was adding complexity rather than value. That part is still entirely human.

If I could go back, I'd do user research before designing a single screen. I made assumptions about what "feeling overwhelmed by money" means based on intuition and observation — not interviews. The design might hold up, but I'd want to know for sure, not assume.

What I'm proudest of: the onboarding flow. It's the riskiest part of the design — asking someone to answer personal questions before they trust you. Getting the tone right took iteration, and I think it landed.
